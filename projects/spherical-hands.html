TARGET: Reproduce and analyze the Spherical Hands underactuated grasping mechanism from Ma et al. (2016) — a robotic hand where all joint axes intersect at a single remote center point, enabling size-invariant grasping without active control of individual finger forces.

APPROACH: Full SolidWorks CAD model with 45-degree angular offsets and 0.72 radian base tilt, Grübler mobility analysis, forward kinematics via Screw Theory and Product of Exponentials, and dynamic Motion Study simulation with passive spring compliance to validate adaptive underactuated grasping.

--- ## SYSTEM_ARCHITECTURE ### [ MECHANISM DESIGN ]
  • Fingers: Two curved fingers + one straight thumb. Each curved finger has two revolute joints with axes intentionally offset at 45° so both intersect at remote center point $N$ above the palm.
  • Underactuation: 3 active proximal joints (motors) drive 6 joints total via passive distal joints with torsional springs — 2:1 underactuation ratio.
  • Thumb: Conventional planar geometry with a passive pivot base adding one extra passive DOF for lateral grasp adaptability without active control.
  • Spring Compliance: Distal joints have $k = 20\;\text{N·mm/deg} \approx 1.15\;\text{N·m/rad}$, damping $c = 0.001\;\text{N·mm/(deg/s)}$.
### [ MOBILITY ANALYSIS (GRÜBLER) ]

Two configurations were analyzed using the spatial Grübler formula $M = 6(n-1-j) + \sum f_i$:

  • Open Hand: $M = 6$ — full spatial mobility for finger positioning.
  • Grasped Object: $M = 3$ — constrained by object contact, enabling stable in-hand manipulation.
--- ## EXECUTION_LOG ### 1. Forward Kinematics — Screw Theory & Product of Exponentials

Each curved finger's proximal and distal joints are modeled as screw axes passing through the remote center point $N$. The screw axis for joint $i$ with unit direction $\omega_i$ is:

$$\mathcal{S}_i = \begin{bmatrix} \omega_i \\ -\omega_i \times r_N \end{bmatrix}$$

The 45-degree angular offsets ensure both axes satisfy the intersection condition $\omega \cdot (N - q) = 0$. The full fingertip transform uses the Product of Exponentials:

$$T(\theta_1, \theta_2) = e^{[\mathcal{S}_1]\theta_1}\,e^{[\mathcal{S}_2]\theta_2}\,M$$

where matrix exponentials follow Rodrigues' formula:

$$e^{[\omega]\theta} = I + \sin\theta\,[\omega] + (1-\cos\theta)\,[\omega]^2$$
### 2. Motion Study — Sinusoidal Motor Trajectories

Three proximal motors follow sinusoidal trajectories with 120° phase offsets, producing a sequential wave-like closing pattern that distributes contact timing across the three fingers:

motion_profile.m ● ACTIVE 
% Sinusoidal motor trajectories with 120-deg phase spacing
f  = 0.5;   % Hz
A  = 20;    % deg amplitude
t0 = 40;    % deg initial angle

theta_F1(t)    = t0 + A * sin(2*pi*f*t +   0);   % phi_1 = 0
theta_F2(t)    = t0 + A * sin(2*pi*f*t + 120);   % phi_2 = 120 deg
theta_thumb(t) = t0 + A * sin(2*pi*f*t + 240);   % phi_3 = 240 deg

% Distal spring dynamics (passive compliance)
tau_spring = -k*(theta - theta0) - c*(d_theta/dt);
% k = 1.15 N.m/rad, c = 0.001 N.mm/(deg/s)
--- ## RESULTS

The Motion Study simulation successfully demonstrated stable grasping of an equilateral triangular prism (inscribing radius $r = 25\;\text{mm}$, depth $d = 10\;\text{mm}$). The passive distal springs automatically adapted to the object geometry, with spring deflections varying per-finger based on contact location and force distribution. The 120° phase spacing produced sequential finger engagement, allowing progressive spring adaptation before full three-point contact was established.

This confirms the geometric design principle: all joint axes intersecting at $N$ creates a spherical workspace constraint that decouples finger motion from object geometry — the mechanism grasps stably regardless of where along the spherical surface contact occurs.

--- ## ANOMALY_REPORT

CHALLENGE: 45-Degree Offset Geometry Complexity

ISSUE: The 45-degree angular offsets required for joint axis intersection at the remote center point $N$ create complex non-planar spatial geometry. Maintaining precise angular relationships throughout the SolidWorks assembly — especially at mating surfaces between proximal links and curved finger geometry — required careful constraint management to avoid over-constrained or degenerate configurations.

KEY TRADE-OFF: The spring constant $k = 1.15\;\text{N·m/rad}$ balances grasp force capability against passive adaptation compliance. Too stiff and the distal joints cannot adapt to object geometry; too compliant and grasp stability is lost under load.

VALIDATION GAP: The Ma et al. reference work validated size-invariant grasping across 38–58 mm object sizes. This implementation demonstrated grasping for a single 25 mm test object. Testing across a range of object sizes remains as future work to fully verify the size-invariance property.
